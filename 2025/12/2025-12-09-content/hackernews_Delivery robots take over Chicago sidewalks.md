LAKEVIEW ‚Äî The robot revolution is here ‚Äî on North Side sidewalks, at least.

With names like Stacey, Quincy and Rajesh, the boxy food delivery robots are regularly zooming down side streets ‚Äî and occasionally getting stuck in the snow ‚Äî to deliver Shake Shack or Taco Bell to eager patrons in Lakeview, Lincoln Park and Uptown, among other neighborhoods. They‚Äôre adorable to some, a safety hazard to others and impossible to ignore for most.

The buzzing bots are causing a stir in person and [online](https://www.reddit.com/r/chicago/comments/1pe9r72/idiot_clanker_doesnt_know_how_to_handle_the_snow/). In neighborhood Facebook groups, they‚Äôve found fervent support and fierce opposition, while a passionate contingent of neighbors have banded together to oust them from the city altogether.

Josh Robertson is leading that charge. The Lincoln Park resident [has launched a petition](https://nosidewalkbots.org/) calling for the city to hit pause on the robots, arguing, ‚ÄúChicago sidewalks are for people, not delivery robots.‚Äù

The petition asks the city‚Äôs transportation and business departments to ‚Äúrelease safety & ADA findings, evaluate that data and local job impacts in a public hearing, and set clear rules‚Äù for the robots. As of Dec. 2, more than 1,500 people have signed the petition, 350 of whom included an ‚Äúincident report‚Äù describing their interactions with the robots.

Robertson said he first noticed the robots in his neighborhood earlier this year and thought they were ‚Äúkind of neat ‚Ä¶ . It felt futuristic.‚Äù

That changed when he went for a walk with his young children and a robot approached on the sidewalk, he said.

‚ÄúThis is a vehicle in the pedestrian path space that‚Äôs meant for people, and yet we ended up stepping aside, and something about that felt a little off,‚Äù Robertson said. ‚ÄúI began to wonder, what are our sidewalks going to be like if these programs are successful from the company‚Äôs point of view, and they continue to scale, and there are dozens and dozens of them on our sidewalks, even on quiet residential sidewalks?‚Äù

The delivery robot pilot program [launched in Chicago in 2022](https://www.chicago.gov/city/en/depts/mayor/press_room/press_releases/2022/july/PersonalDeliveryDevicePilotProgramIntroducedCityCouncil.html) under then-Mayor Lori Lightfoot, and a few companies now operate app-based robots in the city. Coco rolled out last year in the 27th and 34 wards, which include parts of the Loop, Near North Side, West Loop, Near West Side, West Town and West Humboldt Park. The company recently partnered with burger chain Shake Shack.

Serve Robotics, used by UberEats and other food delivery apps, expanded to Chicago in late September. Serve rolled out in partnership with more than 100 restaurants in 14 Chicago neighborhoods, including East Garfield Park, Logan Square and Belmont Cragin.

‚ÄúAbout half of all food deliveries globally are shorter than 2 and a half miles, which basically means that all of our cities are filled with burrito taxis,‚Äù said Viggy Ram, Serve‚Äôs vice president of policy. ‚ÄúThis is really an effort to make short-distance delivery safer, more sustainable and reduce congestion overall.‚Äù

Serve is aware of the Chicago petition and welcomes the feedback, good and bad, in hopes the company can serve the city as best as it can, Ram said. The company‚Äôs goal is to expand into more neighborhoods, he said.

Each bot has a ‚Äúcontact us‚Äù label for those who want to offer feedback, Ram said.
